Date vs. InternalDate

When working with emails in IMAP, you may come across two types of dates: Date and InternalDate. While they might seem similar, they serve different purposes. Here's a simple explanation to help you understand the difference:

1. Date​

  • What it is:
    The Date is the timestamp set by the sender's email client or server when the email was created or sent.
  • Example:
    If you send an email on October 1, 2023, at 10:00 AM, the Date field will reflect that exact time.
  • Why it matters:
    This is the date you typically see in your email client (e.g., Gmail or Outlook) when viewing emails. It helps you know when the email was originally sent.

2. InternalDate​

  • What it is:
    The InternalDate is the timestamp set by the email server when the email was received or stored in your mailbox.
  • Example:
    If the email was sent on October 1, 2023, at 10:00 AM but was delayed and only arrived in your mailbox on October 2, 2023, at 9:00 AM, the InternalDate will show October 2, 2023, at 9:00 AM.
  • Why it matters:
    This date is useful for tracking when the email actually arrived in your mailbox, especially if there are delays in delivery.

Key Differences​

AspectDateInternalDate
Set bySender's email client or serverYour email server
RepresentsWhen the email was sentWhen the email was received
Use caseShows the original sending timeTracks when the email arrived

Real-World Example​

Imagine you send a letter via postal mail:

  • Date: The date you wrote on the letter (e.g., October 1).
  • InternalDate: The date the post office delivered it to the recipient (e.g., October 5).

In emails, the Date is like the date you wrote the email, and the InternalDate is like the date it arrived in the recipient's inbox.

Why Does This Matter?​

  • Sorting emails: If you sort emails by Date, you'll see them in the order they were sent. If you sort by InternalDate, you'll see them in the order they arrived.
  • Troubleshooting delays: If an email arrives late, the InternalDate helps you understand when it actually reached your mailbox.

Which Date Do Most Email Clients Show?​

Most email clients display the Date (the sending timestamp) by default. This is because users are typically more interested in when the email was sent rather than when it arrived on the server.

Why Date is Preferred:​

  • User Expectations: Users care more about when the email was sent, not when it was delivered.
  • Consistency: The Date reflects the original time of the email, regardless of any delays in transmission.

Example:​

  • If an email is sent on October 1, 10:00 AM but arrives on October 2, 9:00 AM due to a delay, the email client will show October 1, 10:00 AM (Date), not October 2, 9:00 AM (InternalDate).

Exceptions:​

Some email clients may show InternalDate in specific cases, such as:

  • Delayed Emails: If an email arrives significantly late, the client might indicate "Delayed" and show the InternalDate.
  • Custom Settings: Some clients allow users to customize which date field to display.
